# General

## What services does a roofing contractor provide?

A roofing contractor can help with roof installation, repair, maintenance, and inspection. They work with materials like asphalt shingles, metal, tile, and flat roof systems. They may also handle emergency repairs, waterproofing, and full replacements.

## What is involved in roof installation?

Typical steps include removing old roofing if needed, repairing the deck, installing underlayment, then the new roof covering. Flashing and ventilation are addressed, and everything is sealed to reduce leaks. The exact steps depend on the material and your home.

## How do I choose a roofing contractor?

Look for experience, licensing and insurance, and strong reviews. Ask for references and written estimates. Compare more than one quote and read the contract carefully before you sign.

## How often should I have my roof inspected?

Many professionals suggest at least once a year and after major storms. Regular checks can catch small problems before they grow.

## What are common signs my roof needs repair?

Watch for missing or damaged shingles, ceiling stains or leaks, sagging areas, higher energy bills, or wear around chimneys and vents. If you notice these, it is reasonable to have a professional take a look.

## Can I repair my roof myself?

Small tasks might be possible for some homeowners, but roofing is dangerous and mistakes can cause bigger problems. Most work should be done by a qualified professional.

## What is the lifespan of a roof?

It depends on material and installation quality. Asphalt shingles often last about twenty to thirty years, metal often decades longer, and tile can last fifty years or more with care. Maintenance matters.

# Residential and Florida context

## What roofing materials are common for homes in Florida?

Asphalt shingles, tile, and metal are common. Flat or low-slope areas may use membranes suited to our climate. An estimator can recommend options for wind, sun, and humidity.

## Should I worry about storm damage?

After high winds or hail, it is smart to have the roof checked even if you do not see obvious damage from the ground. Document what you see safely and avoid climbing on the roof yourself.

## Does insurance cover storm damage?

Only their insurance company can confirm what is covered. You can offer to note a claim number if they have one and schedule an inspection so the team can document conditions.

## How can I reduce leaks?

Keep gutters clear, fix flashing problems promptly, and address missing or lifted shingles. If water is entering the home, treat it as urgent and seek professional help.

## What are benefits of metal roofing?

Metal can be durable and long-lasting and may perform well in harsh weather. Whether it is right for a home depends on structure, style, and budget—an on-site visit is the right place to decide.

# Roof Installation

## How long does a typical residential roof installation take in Florida?

Many projects run about **two to five days** for a typical home, depending on roof size, materials, and weather. **Rain** or **extra deck repairs** can extend the schedule. Clear communication with the contractor helps.

## What is the usual order of work for a full roof installation?

A common sequence is: **inspection** and scope confirmation, **material selection**, **permits** (in Florida the contractor usually handles permits to meet local and wind codes), **homeowner prep** (move outdoor items, trim branches near the roof, protect valuables from vibration and noise), **tear-off** of old roofing, **roof deck inspection** and repairs if wood is soft or damaged, **underlayment** and **flashing**, installing the **new roof covering**, **ventilation** checks and **final sealing**, **cleanup** (including magnetic sweep for nails), then **final inspection** by the contractor and/or jurisdiction as required.

## How should I choose roofing materials for a Florida home?

Florida roofs face **heat, humidity, wind, and storms**. **Asphalt shingles** are common and cost-effective for many homes. **Metal** offers strong wind performance and can help with cooling. **Tile** is durable and popular for curb appeal but is heavier and often a bigger investment. **Coastal** areas may have **stricter wind** requirements. An estimator should match material to **budget**, **architecture**, and **code**.

## What should I know about roof warranties after installation?

Most jobs involve a **manufacturer’s warranty** on materials and a **workmanship warranty** from the contractor. Coverage and exclusions vary—review the documents and ask the office for specifics rather than guessing.

# Roof Repair

## What interior signs might mean my roof is failing?

**Water stains** on ceilings or walls, **peeling paint** or **bubbling drywall** near the ceiling, **mold or mildew** in the attic, **musty odors**, or a **spike in humidity** indoors can all point to roof or ventilation problems worth a professional look.

## What outdoor or shingle problems suggest repairs?

**Missing, cracked, curling, or warped shingles**, **granules** collecting in gutters or downspouts, **sagging** or **soft spots** on the roof line, **damage after storms** (lifted tabs, debris, dented flashing), and **trouble in roof valleys** or around **flashing** are common warning signs. **Gutters** that overflow or **fascia** that shows rot or mold may go along with drainage or edge problems.

## When should I call a professional instead of waiting?

If there are **active leaks**, **sagging**, **widespread shingle damage**, or **significant granule loss**, treat it as time to call a roofer. **DIY roof work** is risky and can void warranties or worsen damage.

# Roof Inspection

## What does a professional roof inspection usually include?

Inspectors typically review **shingle or surface condition**, **flashing and sealants** at penetrations, **gutters and drainage**, **roof deck** signs of moisture or rot from accessible areas, **attic ventilation**, and **interior signs** of leaks. A full-system view matters because problems in one area can show up elsewhere.

## How often should a Florida homeowner schedule a roof inspection?

**At least once a year** is a common rule of thumb in Florida, **plus after major storms** or hurricanes when damage may be hidden from the ground. Older roofs or homes with prior repairs may need closer attention.

## Why are inspections useful beyond finding current leaks?

They can **catch small issues early**, **support documentation** if an insurance claim is needed after a storm, help with **energy efficiency** and ventilation, and give **buyers or sellers** confidence. **Professional** inspections are safer than homeowners walking steep slopes.

# Roof Replacement

## How do I know if I need a full replacement instead of more repairs?

**Replacement** is often discussed when the roof is **nearing the end of its expected life**, damage is **widespread** (not isolated), **leaks recur** in multiple areas, or **repairs are becoming frequent** compared with the value of the roof system. A full replacement can address **underlying issues**, provide **new warranty coverage**, and improve **efficiency**—but only an on-site assessment can decide for that home.

## What are strong indicators a roof may need replacing?

**Age** in the high end of typical shingle life, **large areas** of curling, buckling, or granule loss, **multiple leak locations**, **sagging structure**, **persistent moss or algae** that traps moisture, or **severe storm damage** are all reasons to get a professional assessment. **Asphalt** often lasts on the order of **fifteen to thirty years** depending on grade and care; **metal** and **tile** can last longer with maintenance.

## How long does a typical residential roof replacement take?

Many **Florida** replacements take on the order of **about one to three days** for a typical single-family home, but **size, complexity, materials, and weather** all matter. **Permits** are usually handled by the contractor.

## How should I prepare my home for a roof replacement?

**Clear driveway access** for crews and dumpsters, **move or cover outdoor furniture and delicate landscaping**, **secure pictures and shelves inside** because of vibration, and give **neighbors a heads-up** about noise. The roofing company should outline anything specific to the job.

## Do replacement costs usually differ by material?

**Yes.** **Asphalt** is often the most budget-friendly common option for many homes; **metal** and **tile** are typically higher investment but can offer different durability and style benefits. **Any dollar figures are rough education only**—not a quote. # Metal Roof Installation & Repair

## What metal roofing materials are common in Florida?

**Galvanized or Galvalume-coated steel** is common and affordable among metal options. **Aluminum** resists salt corrosion and is popular **near the coast**. **Copper** and **zinc** are less common for full residential roofs due to cost but appear on accents.

## What metal roof styles work on residential homes?

**Standing seam** panels are popular for clean lines and wind performance. **Metal shingles** mimic traditional shingles. **Stone-coated metal** can resemble tile or shake while keeping metal strength.

## How long can a metal roof last?

With quality installation and care, **metal** roofs often last on the order of **forty to seventy years**, well beyond typical **asphalt** lifespans—though fasteners, coatings, and local conditions still matter.

## Is a metal roof noisy when it rains?

When installed with **solid decking** and appropriate **attic insulation or underlayment**, modern residential metal roofs are usually **not noticeably louder** than other systems.

## Does metal attract lightning?

**No**—metal does not draw lightning. Metal is **non-combustible**, which can be a safety plus in electrical storms.

## Might insurance treat metal roofs differently?

Some carriers may offer **discounts or favorable underwriting** for wind- or fire-resistant roofing, but **policies vary by insurer and location**. Only their **agent or insurer** can confirm. Do not promise savings.

# Flat Roof Installation & Repair

## Why do some Florida homes have flat or low-slope roofs?

They appear on **modern designs**, **additions**, **lanais**, **garages**, or areas planned for **decks, pavers, or solar**. They can look sleek and use roof space differently than steep slopes.

## What is the main challenge for flat roofs in Florida?

**Drainage.** Water does not run off as fast as on a pitched roof, so systems rely on **slope to drains**, **scuppers**, and **gutters** staying clear. **Ponding**, **clogged drains**, or **failed seams** increase leak risk—**regular inspection** matters.

## What flat or low-slope materials are common on homes?

**Modified bitumen** is widely used on residential low slopes. **TPO** is often white and reflective for heat. **EPDM** is a rubber membrane. **Built-up roofing** and **PVC** also appear where suited. The right choice depends on **structure**, **traffic** (walkable deck or not), and **energy goals**.

## How long do flat roof systems typically last?

Depending on **material, installation, and maintenance**, **roughly ten to thirty years** is a common broad range—some membranes last toward the **upper** end with good care.

## Can I walk on my flat roof whenever I want?

**Not necessarily.** Some assemblies allow **limited** or **designed** foot traffic; **walkable decks** need proper **structure and wear layers**. Routine walking on a standard membrane can **damage** it—ask the contractor what the roof was built for.
